Definitions

Definitions

Basing the Design on Task Requirements PDF p.4

Definitions
  • Universally Programmable: Robots are designed to perform a variety of tasks but are often specialized for specific applications. PDF p.4
  • Degrees of Freedom (DOF)The number of independent parameters needed to specify a robot's configuration. Each actuated joint contributes one DOF. A general spatial task requires 6 DOF (3 translation + 3 rotation).Craig, Introduction to Robotics, Ch. 8: The number of DOF should match the number required by the task. Not all tasks require a full six DOF. PDF p.4
  • Redundant (for a task): A robot is considered redundant for a task when the tool has an axis of symmetry, allowing an infinite number of equivalent orientations. PDF p.4
  • Symmetric-Tool Handling: Imagining an extra "fictitious" joint along the tool's axis of symmetry can simplify positioning. Normally requires six degrees of freedom, but with the fictitious joint, only five real joints are needed. PDF p.5
  • Active Positioning Devices: Devices like tilt/roll platforms that reduce the robot's required degrees of freedom. PDF p.6
  • Positioning StructureThe first three joints of a manipulator, which position the wrist point in space. These joints determine the reachable workspace volume and the kinematic configuration class (Cartesian, SCARA, articulated, etc.).Craig, Introduction to Robotics, Ch. 8: The first three joints of a manipulator, which position the wrist point in space. PDF p.9
  • Orienting Structure (Wrist): The last $n - 3$ joints that orient the end-effector and have axes intersecting at the wrist point. PDF p.9
  • Wrist PointThe point where the axes of the last n - 3 joints intersect. When the wrist is spherical (3 intersecting revolute axes), the inverse kinematics can be decoupled into a positioning sub-problem and an orientation sub-problem.Craig, Introduction to Robotics, Ch. 8: The point where the axes of the last $n - 3$ joints intersect. PDF p.9

Worked Examples

Example — Grinding Tool Redundancy
PDF p.4

Problem: A manipulator positions a grinding tool in two different configurations.

Key Insight: The orientation of the tool relative to its axis $\hat{Z}_T$ is irrelevant because the grinding wheel rotates at several hundred RPM. Since the tool can be positioned in an infinite number of ways by rotating around $\hat{Z}_T$, the robot is considered redundant for this task.

Conclusion: Symmetric tools may reduce required DOF. Arc welding, spot welding, deburring, glueing, and polishing often employ end-effectors with at least one axis of symmetry.

Grinding Tool with Manipulator (Ch8-4): Two side-by-side illustrations of a robotic arm holding a grinding wheel positioned against a surface in two different configurations. Both poses achieve equivalent grinding despite different arm configurations, demonstrating the redundancy from tool symmetry about $\hat{Z}_T$.
Example — Circuit Board Assembly
PDF p.5

Problem: Placing components on circuit boards.

Solution: Requires three degrees of freedom for planar movement ($x$, $y$, rotation) and a fourth for lifting/inserting parts ($z$). Total: 4 DOF.

Example — Pipe Welding with Tilt/Roll Platform
PDF p.6

Problem: Welding pipes using an active positioning device.

Solution: A tilt/roll platform accounts for two degrees of freedom, theoretically allowing a 3-DOF robot for symmetric-tool tasks.

Tilt/Roll Platform (Ch8-6): A robotic arm mounted above a tilt/roll platform holding a workpiece. The platform provides two additional degrees of freedom (tilt and roll) to the overall manipulator system.

Theorems & Properties

Properties
  • Many industrial robots have five degrees of freedom (5-DOF) due to the prevalence of symmetric tools. PDF p.5
  • Tasks with fewer than six degrees of freedom are common in industry. PDF p.5
  • Most manipulators are designed so that the last $n - 3$ joints orient the end-effector and have axes that intersect at the wrist point, and the first three joints position this wrist point. PDF p.9

A full 6 DOF is needed to place an end-effector at an arbitrary position and orientation in 3D space. However, many real tasks have inherent symmetries or constraints that reduce this requirement:

  • Symmetric tools (grinders, drills): rotation about the tool axis is irrelevant, removing 1 DOF.
  • Planar tasks (circuit board assembly): motion is restricted to a plane, needing only 3–4 DOF.
  • Active workholding (tilt/roll platforms): the workpiece provides some of the required DOF externally.

Matching DOF to the task keeps the robot simpler, cheaper, faster, and more reliable.

Definitions

PDF p.7

Why SCARA robots are so fast: Because the first three joints do not support the weight of the links or payload (all motion is in the horizontal plane), the actuators can be housed in the stationary base and made very large. This is why some SCARA robots achieve speeds up to 30 ft/s — roughly 10x faster than comparable articulated robots.

Why articulated robots have the best $Q_L$: The articulated configuration sweeps out a roughly spherical workspace with minimal total link length ($Q_L \approx 0.62$), compared to $Q_L = 3$ for a Cartesian robot. This means articulated robots use their structural material most efficiently to create workspace volume.

Cartesian Manipulator PDF p.10

Definition
  • Cartesian ManipulatorA manipulator whose first three joints are prismatic and mutually orthogonal, corresponding to the X, Y, and Z Cartesian directions. Also known as a gantry robot when built at large scale.Craig, Introduction to Robotics, Ch. 8: A manipulator where joints 1 through 3 are prismatic, mutually orthogonal, and correspond to the $X$, $Y$, and $Z$ Cartesian directions.
  • Gantry Robots: Very large Cartesian robots that sometimes manipulate entire automobiles or inspect entire aircraft.
Properties
  • The inverse kinematic solution for this configuration is trivial.
  • This configuration produces robots with very stiff structures. As a consequence, very large robots can be built.
  • The first three joints are decoupled. This makes them simpler to design and prevents kinematic singularities due to the first three joints.

Articulated Manipulator PDF p.11

Definition

Articulated ManipulatorAlso called a jointed, elbow, or anthropomorphic manipulator. It uses revolute joints throughout the positioning structure, producing a roughly spherical workspace and the best structural length index ($Q_L \approx 0.62$).Craig, Introduction to Robotics, Ch. 8: Sometimes also called a jointed, elbow, or anthropomorphic manipulator. Typically consists of two "shoulder" joints (one for rotation about a vertical axis and one for elevation out of the horizontal plane), an "elbow" joint (whose axis is usually parallel to the shoulder elevation joint), and two or three wrist joints at the end.

Properties
  • Articulated robots minimize the intrusion of the manipulator structure into the workspace, making them capable of reaching into confined spaces. PDF p.11
  • They require much less overall structure than Cartesian robots, making them less expensive for applications needing smaller workspaces. PDF p.11

SCARA Manipulator PDF p.12

Definition

SCARA ManipulatorSelective Compliance Assembly Robot Arm. Has three parallel revolute joints for planar motion plus a prismatic joint for vertical motion. Extremely fast because actuators are housed in the stationary base and joints do not fight gravity.Craig, Introduction to Robotics, Ch. 8: Has three parallel revolute joints (allowing it to move and orient in a plane), with a fourth prismatic joint for moving the end-effector normal to the plane.

Properties
  • The first three joints do not have to support any of the weight of the manipulator or the load. PDF p.12
  • Link 0 can easily house the actuators for the first two joints. The actuators can be made very large, so the robot can move very fast. PDF p.12
  • One SCARA manipulator can move at up to 30 feet per second, about 10 times faster than most articulated industrial robots. PDF p.12

Quantitative Measures of Workspace Attributes PDF p.14

Definitions

Length Sum ($L$)A rough measure of the "length" of the complete linkage. For prismatic joints, $d_i$ is interpreted as the length of travel between joint-travel limits. Lower total length for a given workspace volume indicates a more efficient design.Craig, Introduction to Robotics, Ch. 8: A rough measure of the "length" of the complete linkage, defined as:

$$L = \sum_{i=1}^{N} (a_{i-1} + d_i)$$

where $a_{i-1}$ and $d_i$ are the link length and joint offset as defined in Chapter 3. For prismatic joints, $d_i$ must be interpreted as a constant equal to the length of travel between the joint-travel limits. PDF p.14

Structural Length Index ($Q_L$)The ratio of the manipulator's length sum to the cube root of the workspace volume. A dimensionless measure of how efficiently the robot's physical size translates into usable workspace. Good designs have low $Q_L$.Craig, Introduction to Robotics, Ch. 8: Defined as the ratio of the manipulator's length sum to the cube root of the workspace volume:

$$Q_L = \frac{L}{\sqrt[3]{w}}$$

where $w$ is the volume of the manipulator's workspace. Good designs have low $Q_L$. PDF p.14

$Q_L$ captures a fundamental design trade-off: how efficiently does a manipulator's physical size translate into usable workspace?

Consider two robots with the same total link length $L$. The one that sweeps out a larger workspace volume $w$ will have a lower $Q_L$ and is the better design from a workspace-efficiency standpoint. The articulated configuration achieves the theoretical best ($Q_L \approx 0.62$) because revolute joints sweep out spherical volumes, which enclose the most volume for a given radius. In contrast, Cartesian configurations waste structural length on linear rails that only produce rectangular volumes ($Q_L = 3$).

Theorems & Properties

Optimal $Q_L$ Values

Cartesian Manipulator: The value of $Q_L$ is minimized when all three joints have the same length of travel. This minimal value is $Q_L = 3$. PDF p.15

Articulated Manipulator:

$$Q_L = \frac{1}{\sqrt[3]{4\pi/3}} \cong 0.62$$ PDF p.15

Example — SCARA $Q_L$ Optimization
PDF p.15

Problem: A SCARA manipulator has links 1 and 2 of equal length $l/2$, and the range of motion of prismatic joint 3 is given by $d_3$. Assume joint limits are absent. Find $Q_L$. What value of $d_3$ minimizes $Q_L$ and what is this minimal value?

Step 1: Compute the length sum
$$L = \frac{l}{2} + \frac{l}{2} + d_3 = l + d_3$$
Step 2: Compute the workspace volume

The workspace is a right cylinder of radius $l$ and height $d_3$. Therefore:

$$w = \pi l^2 d_3$$
Step 3: Write $Q_L$
$$Q_L = \frac{l + d_3}{\sqrt[3]{\pi l^2 d_3}}$$
Step 4: Minimize $Q_L$ as a function of $d_3 / l$

Optimization gives $d_3 = \dfrac{l}{2}$ as the optimal value.

Step 5: Compute the minimal $Q_L$

The corresponding minimal value of $Q_L$ is 1.29.

Enrichment: Derivation of Optimal $Q_L$ for Cartesian and Articulated Configurations
Enrichment — Derivation

Cartesian manipulator: For a Cartesian robot with three prismatic joints of travel $d_1$, $d_2$, $d_3$:

  • $L = d_1 + d_2 + d_3$
  • $w = d_1 \cdot d_2 \cdot d_3$
  • By the AM-GM inequality, for a fixed $L$, the product $d_1 d_2 d_3$ is maximized when $d_1 = d_2 = d_3 = L/3$.
  • Then $w = (L/3)^3 = L^3/27$, so $Q_L = L / \sqrt[3]{L^3/27} = L / (L/3) = 3$.

Articulated manipulator: An idealized articulated arm of total length $L$ (single link, full joint range) sweeps out a sphere of radius $L$:

  • $w = \frac{4}{3}\pi L^3$
  • $Q_L = \frac{L}{\sqrt[3]{\frac{4}{3}\pi L^3}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt[3]{\frac{4\pi}{3}}} \approx 0.62$

This shows that articulated configurations are roughly 5x more workspace-efficient than Cartesian configurations.

Enrichment: Condition Number and Isotropy
Enrichment — Condition Number

Beyond the manipulability measure $\mathcal{w}$, the condition number of the Jacobian provides a complementary measure of workspace quality.

Condition number: Defined as the ratio of the largest to smallest singular value of the Jacobian:

$$\kappa(\mathbf{J}) = \frac{\sigma_{\max}}{\sigma_{\min}}$$
  • $\kappa = 1$: The manipulability ellipsoid is a sphere (isotropic configuration). The robot can move equally well in all directions.
  • $\kappa \to \infty$: The robot is at or near a singularity. The ellipsoid collapses toward a line or plane.

The isotropy index is the reciprocal:

$$\mu = \frac{1}{\kappa(\mathbf{J})} = \frac{\sigma_{\min}}{\sigma_{\max}} \in [0, 1]$$

A value of $\mu = 1$ represents perfect isotropy. Designing a manipulator so that a large portion of its workspace has $\mu$ close to 1 ensures well-conditioned operation throughout.

Key distinction: The manipulability measure $\mathcal{w}$ tells you the overall magnitude of velocity capability (volume of the ellipsoid), while the condition number tells you the uniformity of that capability (shape of the ellipsoid). A robot can have high $\mathcal{w}$ but poor conditioning if it can move very fast in one direction but barely at all in another.

Manipulability Measure PDF p.18

Definitions

Singular Configurations: Found by solving $\det(\mathbf{J}(\Theta)) = 0$.

Manipulability Measure ($\mathcal{w}$)A scalar measure of manipulator dexterity defined by Yoshikawa (1985). It equals the product of all singular values of the Jacobian, which is proportional to the volume of the velocity ellipsoid. At singularities, $\mathcal{w} = 0$. Good designs maximize $\mathcal{w}$ over large regions of the workspace.Yoshikawa, T. "Manipulability of Robotic Mechanisms." IJRR, 4(2), 1985: A measure of manipulator dexterity defined as:

$$\mathcal{w} = \sqrt{\det(\mathbf{J}(\Theta)\,\mathbf{J}^\top(\Theta))}$$

which, for nonredundant manipulators, reduces to:

$$\mathcal{w} = |\det(\mathbf{J}(\Theta))|$$ PDF p.18
Properties
  • A good manipulator design has large areas of its workspace characterized by high values of $\mathcal{w}$. PDF p.18
  • At singular configurations, $\mathcal{w} = 0$. PDF p.18

At a singularity, $\det(\mathbf{J}) = 0$, which means $\mathbf{J}$ has at least one zero singular value. Since $\mathcal{w} = \prod \sigma_i$, any zero singular value makes the entire product zero. Geometrically, the velocity ellipsoid collapses from a full-dimensional ellipsoid to a lower-dimensional object (e.g., a disc or a line), meaning the robot cannot move in at least one direction.

Enrichment: Yoshikawa's Manipulability Measure — Geometric Derivation
Enrichment — Proof

Yoshikawa's manipulability measure (1985) has a clean geometric interpretation as the volume of the velocity ellipsoid. Here is the derivation.

Setup

The relationship between joint velocities and end-effector velocities is:

$$\dot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{J}(\Theta)\,\dot{\Theta}$$

Consider all joint velocities $\dot{\Theta}$ satisfying the unit-norm constraint $\|\dot{\Theta}\| \leq 1$. This defines a unit hypersphere in joint-velocity space.

Step 1: Mapping through the Jacobian

The image of this unit sphere under the linear map $\mathbf{J}$ is an ellipsoid in task space, defined by:

$$\mathcal{E} = \left\{ \dot{\mathbf{x}} \in \mathbb{R}^m \;\middle|\; \dot{\mathbf{x}}^\top \left(\mathbf{J}\mathbf{J}^\top\right)^{-1} \dot{\mathbf{x}} \leq 1 \right\}$$

This is the manipulability ellipsoid (or velocity ellipsoid).

Step 2: Singular Value Decomposition

Let $\mathbf{J} = \mathbf{U}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{V}^\top$ be the SVD, where $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \text{diag}(\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \ldots, \sigma_m)$ contains the singular values. Then:

$$\mathbf{J}\mathbf{J}^\top = \mathbf{U}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^2\mathbf{U}^\top$$

The eigenvalues of $\mathbf{J}\mathbf{J}^\top$ are $\sigma_1^2, \sigma_2^2, \ldots, \sigma_m^2$, and the eigenvectors (columns of $\mathbf{U}$) give the principal axes of the ellipsoid. The semi-axis lengths are $\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \ldots, \sigma_m$.

Step 3: Volume of the ellipsoid

The volume of an $m$-dimensional ellipsoid with semi-axes $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_m$ is:

$$V_{\mathcal{E}} = C_m \prod_{i=1}^{m} \sigma_i$$

where $C_m$ is the volume of the unit $m$-ball ($C_2 = \pi$, $C_3 = \frac{4\pi}{3}$, etc.).

Step 4: Connecting to the determinant

Since:

$$\prod_{i=1}^{m} \sigma_i = \sqrt{\prod_{i=1}^{m} \sigma_i^2} = \sqrt{\det(\mathbf{J}\mathbf{J}^\top)}$$

The manipulability measure is:

$$\mathcal{w} = \sqrt{\det(\mathbf{J}\mathbf{J}^\top)} = \prod_{i=1}^{m} \sigma_i$$

Thus, $\mathcal{w}$ is proportional to the volume of the velocity ellipsoid. When $\mathcal{w} = 0$, at least one singular value is zero, the ellipsoid collapses to a lower-dimensional object, and the robot has lost the ability to move in at least one direction.

Step 5: Nonredundant case

For nonredundant manipulators ($n = m$, so $\mathbf{J}$ is square):

$$\det(\mathbf{J}\mathbf{J}^\top) = \det(\mathbf{J})^2 \implies \mathcal{w} = |\det(\mathbf{J})|$$
Step 1 of 6

Manipulability via Acceleration Analysis — Asada's Approach PDF p.19

Definitions

Cartesian Mass MatrixThe effective inertia matrix experienced at the end-effector in Cartesian space. It is obtained by transforming the joint-space mass matrix through the inverse Jacobian. Its eigenvalues and eigenvectors define the inertia ellipsoid.Asada, H. 1983, 1984: Defined as:

$$\mathbf{M}_x(\Theta) = \mathbf{J}^{-\top}(\Theta)\,\mathbf{M}(\Theta)\,\mathbf{J}^{-1}(\Theta)$$

This serves as a measure of how well the manipulator can accelerate in various Cartesian directions. PDF p.19

Inertia EllipsoidA graphical representation of the Cartesian mass matrix. Short axes indicate directions of high effective inertia (poor acceleration), while long axes indicate directions of low effective inertia (easy acceleration). This is the opposite sense from the velocity ellipsoid.Asada, H. 1983, 1984: A graphic representation of the Cartesian mass matrix, given by:

$$\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{M}_x(\Theta)\,\mathbf{X} = 1$$ PDF p.19
Properties
  • The inertia ellipsoid equation represents an $n$-dimensional ellipse, where $n$ is the dimension of $\mathbf{X}$. PDF p.19
  • The axes of the ellipsoid lie in the directions of the eigenvectors of $\mathbf{M}_x(\Theta)$. PDF p.19
  • The reciprocals of the square roots of the corresponding eigenvalues provide the lengths of the axes of the ellipsoids. PDF p.19
  • Well-conditioned points in the manipulator workspace are characterized by inertia ellipsoids that are spherical (or nearly so). PDF p.19

For the velocity ellipsoid, long axes mean the robot can achieve high velocities in that direction. For the inertia ellipsoid, long axes (small eigenvalues of $\mathbf{M}_x$) mean low effective inertia, so the robot accelerates easily in that direction.

The inversion arises because the inertia ellipsoid is defined by $\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{M}_x \mathbf{X} = 1$, where large $\mathbf{M}_x$ eigenvalues shrink the ellipsoid axis. The force ellipsoid $\mathbf{F}^\top (\mathbf{J}\mathbf{J}^\top) \mathbf{F} = 1$ is similarly inversely related to the velocity ellipsoid: directions where the robot moves fast are directions where it can only exert small forces, and vice versa.

Worked Example — Planar 2-Link Inertia Ellipsoids

Example — Planar Two-Link RR Manipulator Inertia Ellipsoids

Problem: Consider a planar two-link RR manipulator. Find the inertia ellipsoids across the workspace.

Enrichment — Derivation

Asada's approach (1983, 1984) extends the classical concept of a single rigid body's inertia ellipsoid to an entire articulated chain.

Starting point — Joint-space dynamics (ignoring gravity and velocity terms):

$$\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{M}(\Theta)\,\ddot{\Theta}$$

Step 1 — Transform to Cartesian space: Using $\dot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{J}\dot{\Theta}$, differentiate to get:

$$\ddot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{J}\ddot{\Theta} + \dot{\mathbf{J}}\dot{\Theta}$$

At a snapshot (ignoring the $\dot{\mathbf{J}}\dot{\Theta}$ term for the quasi-static analysis), we have $\ddot{\Theta} = \mathbf{J}^{-1}\ddot{\mathbf{x}}$.

Step 2 — Substitute into dynamics:

$$\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{M}\mathbf{J}^{-1}\ddot{\mathbf{x}}$$

Using the virtual work principle ($\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{J}^{-\top}\boldsymbol{\tau}$), the Cartesian force is:

$$\mathbf{F} = \underbrace{\mathbf{J}^{-\top}\mathbf{M}\mathbf{J}^{-1}}_{\mathbf{M}_x}\;\ddot{\mathbf{x}}$$

Step 3 — Interpret the ellipsoid: The set of all Cartesian accelerations achievable with unit joint torque ($\|\boldsymbol{\tau}\| \leq 1$) forms an ellipsoid whose shape is governed by $\mathbf{M}_x^{-1}$. Equivalently, the equation $\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{M}_x \mathbf{X} = 1$ defines the inertia ellipsoid.

Physical meaning of the ellipsoid axes:

  • Short axis (large eigenvalue of $\mathbf{M}_x$): The direction in which the robot has high effective inertia and therefore accelerates poorly.
  • Long axis (small eigenvalue of $\mathbf{M}_x$): The direction in which the robot has low effective inertia and accelerates easily.

Design implication: By plotting inertia ellipsoids across the workspace at the design stage, engineers can optimize link masses, lengths, and mass distributions so that the ellipsoids remain as spherical as possible throughout the intended working region.

Stewart Mechanism Properties
  • Actuator Connections: Actuators are connected to the base with two-degree-of-freedom universal joints and to the end-effector with three-degree-of-freedom ball-and-socket joints. PDF p.21
  • Characteristics: The mechanism is very stiff due to its closed-loop structure but has a more limited range of motion compared to serial linkages. PDF p.21
  • Kinematic Solutions: The inverse kinematic solution is simple, whereas the forward kinematic solution is complex and often lacks a closed-form formulation. PDF p.21
Stewart Mechanism (Ch8-21): A 3D illustration of a Stewart platform (hexapod). The top platform (end-effector) is connected to the base by six linear actuators ($d_1$ through $d_6$) arranged symmetrically. Universal joints (2-DOF) at the base, ball-and-socket joints (3-DOF) at the end-effector.

In a serial chain, forward kinematics is a simple chain of matrix multiplications, while inverse kinematics requires solving a nonlinear system. In a parallel chain, the structure is reversed:

  • Inverse kinematics (simple): Given the desired pose, each actuator length $d_i$ is simply the Euclidean distance between its base and platform attachment points after applying the rigid-body transformation: $$d_i = \| \mathbf{p}_{\text{base},i} - (\mathbf{R}\,\mathbf{p}_{\text{platform},i} + \mathbf{t}) \|$$ This is a closed-form solution requiring only basic vector arithmetic.
  • Forward kinematics (complex): Given six actuator lengths, finding the resulting platform pose requires solving a system of six nonlinear equations simultaneously. The general Stewart platform has been shown to have up to 40 solutions.

Enrichment: Redundant Manipulators — Self-Motion and Practical Benefits
Enrichment — Redundancy

Why more than 6 DOF? A 6-DOF serial manipulator generally has a finite number of inverse kinematic solutions for a given end-effector pose. A redundant manipulator ($n > 6$) has infinitely many solutions, forming a self-motion manifold.

Self-motion: Holding the end-effector pose constant while continuously moving through joint configurations. For a 7-DOF arm, this commonly appears as the elbow tracing a circle around the line from shoulder to wrist, analogous to how a human can move their elbow up or down while keeping their hand fixed.

Practical advantages of redundancy:

  1. Singularity avoidance: The extra DOF allows the robot to "steer around" singular configurations.
  2. Obstacle avoidance: The robot can reconfigure its intermediate links to avoid collisions.
  3. Joint limit avoidance: Self-motion keeps all joints away from their mechanical limits.
  4. Torque/force optimization: Null-space motion can minimize joint torques.
  5. Fault tolerance: If one joint fails, the robot may still complete the task.

Mathematical formulation:

$$\dot{\Theta} = \mathbf{J}^{\dagger}\dot{\mathbf{x}} + (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{J}^{\dagger}\mathbf{J})\mathbf{z}$$

where $\mathbf{J}^{\dagger}$ is the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse and $\mathbf{z}$ is an arbitrary vector. The term $(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{J}^{\dagger}\mathbf{J})\mathbf{z}$ projects $\mathbf{z}$ into the null space of $\mathbf{J}$, producing joint velocities that do not affect the end-effector.

Industrial example: The KUKA LBR iiwa is a well-known 7-DOF collaborative robot. Its extra DOF enables human-safe operation in cluttered environments.

Key Concepts — Chapter 8
Structural Length Index
What is $Q_L$ and what does a low value indicate?
Click to flip
Answer
$Q_L = \dfrac{L}{\sqrt[3]{w}}$, the ratio of the manipulator's length sum to the cube root of workspace volume. A low $Q_L$ indicates efficient use of structural material to create workspace volume. The articulated configuration achieves the best value ($Q_L \approx 0.62$).
Manipulability Measure
How is Yoshikawa's manipulability measure $\mathcal{w}$ defined, and what does it represent geometrically?
Click to flip
Answer
$\mathcal{w} = \sqrt{\det(\mathbf{J}\mathbf{J}^\top)} = \prod \sigma_i$

It is proportional to the volume of the velocity ellipsoid. When $\mathcal{w} = 0$, the robot is at a singularity. For nonredundant robots: $\mathcal{w} = |\det(\mathbf{J})|$.
Cartesian Mass Matrix
What is the Cartesian mass matrix and how does the inertia ellipsoid differ from the velocity ellipsoid?
Click to flip
Answer
$\mathbf{M}_x = \mathbf{J}^{-\top}\mathbf{M}\mathbf{J}^{-1}$

The inertia ellipsoid ($\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{M}_x \mathbf{X} = 1$) has the opposite sense from the velocity ellipsoid: long axes mean easy acceleration (low inertia), while short axes mean poor acceleration (high inertia).
SCARA Configuration
Why are SCARA robots so much faster than articulated robots?
Click to flip
Answer
SCARA robots move in the horizontal plane, so joints do not fight gravity. The actuators for the first two joints are housed in the stationary base and can be made very large. Some SCARA robots achieve speeds up to 30 ft/s — roughly 10x faster than articulated robots.
Redundant Manipulators
What is the general inverse velocity solution for a redundant manipulator, and what does the null-space term do?
Click to flip
Answer
$\dot{\Theta} = \mathbf{J}^{\dagger}\dot{\mathbf{x}} + (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{J}^{\dagger}\mathbf{J})\mathbf{z}$

The first term gives the minimum-norm solution. The null-space term $(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{J}^{\dagger}\mathbf{J})\mathbf{z}$ projects $\mathbf{z}$ into the null space of $\mathbf{J}$, producing joint velocities that do not affect the end-effector but can be used for singularity avoidance, obstacle avoidance, etc.
Stewart Platform
Why is inverse kinematics simple but forward kinematics complex for a Stewart platform?
Click to flip
Answer
IK (simple): Each actuator length is just the distance between its base and platform attachment points: $d_i = \|\mathbf{p}_{\text{base},i} - (\mathbf{R}\mathbf{p}_{\text{platform},i} + \mathbf{t})\|$.

FK (complex): Given 6 lengths, finding the platform pose requires solving 6 simultaneous nonlinear equations with up to 40 solutions.
