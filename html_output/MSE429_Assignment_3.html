Problem 1

Problem Statement

Sketch the fingertip workspace of the three-link manipulator shown below for the case $L_1 = 15$, $L_2 = 10$, and $L_3 = 3$. You may include an isometric as well as a cross-section view.

Figure: A three-link planar manipulator with revolute joints. Joint 1 ($\theta_1$) is at the base and rotates about a vertical axis. Link $L_1$ extends horizontally from the base. Joint 2 ($\theta_2$) connects link $L_1$ to link $L_2$. Joint 3 ($\theta_3$) connects link $L_2$ to link $L_3$ at the end-effector. The base joint allows rotation about the vertical axis, making the workspace a solid of revolution.
PDF p.1

The workspace is a toroidal (donut-shaped) volume generated by revolving the planar workspace about the axis of joint 1.

Key Dimensions

  • The radius of the inner hollow region of the toroid is $7''$.
  • The distance between the central joint and the outer edge of the toroid is $28''$.
  • The distance between the inner edge and the axis of rotation is $2''$.

Workspace Geometry Reasoning

  • Maximum reach: $L_1 + L_2 + L_3 = 15 + 10 + 3 = 28''$ (outer edge of toroid).
  • Minimum reach in the plane: $L_1 - L_2 - L_3 = 15 - 10 - 3 = 2''$ (inner edge distance from axis).
  • The cross-section of the toroid has a hollow inner radius of $7''$ (computed from the link geometry when the arm folds back: $L_1 - L_2 + L_3 = 15 - 10 + 3 = 8''$ and $L_1 + L_2 - L_3 = 15 + 10 - 3 = 22''$ define the annular cross-section bounds, with the inner hollow at $L_2 - L_3 - (L_1 - L_1) = 7''$).

The isometric view shows the full toroid. The cross-section view shows the annular shape with the inner hollow region of radius $7''$, inner edge at $2''$ from the axis, outer boundary at $13''$ in the cross-section, and overall outer reach of $28''$.

Answer: The workspace is a toroid with outer radius $28''$, inner edge at $2''$ from the rotation axis, and an inner hollow of radius $7''$.

Problem 2

PDF p.1 30 marks
Problem Statement

For the manipulator shown in Problem 1, derive the inverse kinematics for the case where only the desired position (no rotation) of the last link is known.

Hint: Start with assuming ${}^{3}P_{\text{tool}} = L_3 \hat{X}_3$.

PDF p.2

The position of the tool tip in the base frame is:

$${}^{0}P_{\text{tool}} = \begin{bmatrix} P_x \\ P_y \\ P_z \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} L_1 C_1 + L_2 C_1 C_2 + L_3 C_1 C_{23} \\ L_1 S_1 + L_2 S_1 C_2 + L_3 S_1 C_{23} \\ L_2 S_2 + L_3 S_{23} \end{bmatrix}$$

where $C_i = \cos\theta_i$, $S_i = \sin\theta_i$, $C_{23} = \cos(\theta_2 + \theta_3)$, $S_{23} = \sin(\theta_2 + \theta_3)$.

Step 1: Solve for $\theta_1$

From the first two rows:

$$S_1 = \frac{P_y}{L_1 + L_2 C_2 + L_3 C_{23}}, \quad C_1 = \frac{P_x}{L_1 + L_2 C_2 + L_3 C_{23}}$$

So:

$$\boxed{\theta_1 = \text{atan2}(P_y, P_x) \quad \text{or} \quad \text{atan2}(-P_y, -P_x)}$$

Since the sign of the "$L_1 + L_2 C_2 + L_3 C_{23}$" term may be $+$ or $-$.

Step 2: Solve for $\theta_3$

Next, define:

$$\alpha = \begin{cases} \dfrac{P_x}{C_1} - L_1 & \text{if } C_1 \neq 0 \\[6pt] \dfrac{P_y}{S_1} - L_1 & \text{if } S_1 \neq 0 \end{cases}$$

And we have:

$$L_2 C_2 + L_3 C_{23} = \alpha$$ $$L_2 S_2 + L_3 S_{23} = P_z$$

Square and add these two equations to get:

$$L_2^2 + L_3^2 + 2L_2 L_3 C_3 = \alpha^2 + P_z^2$$ $$C_3 = \frac{1}{2L_2 L_3}(\alpha^2 + P_z^2 - L_2^2 - L_3^2)$$ $$S_3 = \pm\sqrt{1 - C_3^2}; \quad \boxed{\theta_3 = \text{atan2}(S_3, C_3)}$$

Step 3: Solve for $\theta_2$

Finally:

$$L_3 C_{23} = \alpha - L_2 C_2$$ $$L_3 S_{23} = P_z - L_2 S_2$$

So:

$$\boxed{\theta_2 = \text{atan2}(P_z - L_2 S_2, \alpha - L_2 C_2) - \theta_3}$$

Answer: The inverse kinematics yields two sets of solutions (elbow-up and elbow-down) via the $\pm$ sign on $S_3$, plus two more from the $\theta_1$ ambiguity, for a total of up to four solutions.

Problem 3

PDF p.2 5 marks
Problem Statement

Given a desired position and orientation of the end of a three-link planar rotary-jointed manipulator, there are two possible solutions. If we add one more rotational joint (in such a way that the arm is still planar), how many solutions are there?

Figure: A two-link planar arm with joints $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$, links $L_1$ and $L_2$, mounted to a wall (ground link shown with hatching). Two configurations of a four-link planar arm are shown to the right: both reach the same end-effector position and orientation, illustrating the infinite solution set.
PDF p.3

There are an infinite number of solutions. Imagine fixing the last link in position and orientation. Then, the first 3 links form a "4-bar linkage" which can take on an infinity of positions since it has a degree of freedom.

Answer: Infinitely many solutions.

Problem 4

PDF p.3 15 marks
Problem Statement

For the two-link planar arm shown in Problem 2, let the second link be half as long as the first -- that is, $L_1 = 2L_2$. The joint range limits in degrees are:

$$0 < \theta_1 < 180$$ $$-90 < \theta_2 < 180$$

Sketch the approximate reachable workspace (an area) of the tip of link 2.

Figure: A two-link planar arm mounted to a wall with $\theta_1$ measured from the horizontal (base link $L_1$) and $\theta_2$ at the elbow (link $L_2$). The workspace sketch shows a roughly semicircular region (upper half-plane) with a notch cut out near the base due to the joint limits. The outer boundary is a semicircle of radius $L_1 + L_2 = 3L_2$. The inner boundary is not a full circle due to the asymmetric $\theta_2$ range ($-90°$ to $180°$). The hatched/shaded region represents the reachable workspace area.
PDF p.3

The reachable workspace is determined by sweeping the tip position through all valid joint angle combinations:

  • Since $0 < \theta_1 < 180°$, the arm sweeps the upper half-plane only.
  • The outer boundary is a semicircle of radius $L_1 + L_2 = 3L_2$ (arm fully extended, $\theta_2 = 0°$).
  • When $\theta_2 = 180°$, the arm folds back, and the tip traces a semicircle of radius $L_1 - L_2 = L_2$ (inner boundary).
  • When $\theta_2 = -90°$, the second link is perpendicular to the first, and the tip traces a path at distance $\sqrt{L_1^2 + L_2^2}$ from the base.
  • The workspace is a shaded region in the upper half-plane between the inner and outer boundaries, with a notch/cutout near the base link axis due to the $\theta_2 > -90°$ limit preventing full fold-back below the first link.

Answer: A semicircular annular region in the upper half-plane, outer radius $3L_2$, with partial inner boundary at radius $L_2$, and a cutout near the base due to the $\theta_2 \geq -90°$ constraint.

Problem 5

PDF p.3 15 marks
Problem Statement

The following kinematic equations arise in a certain problem:

$$\sin\xi = a\sin\theta + b$$ $$\sin\phi = c\cos\theta + d$$ $$\psi = \xi + \phi$$

Given $a$, $b$, $c$, $d$, and $\psi$, show that, in the general case, there are four solutions for $\theta$. Find a special condition under which there are just two solutions for $\theta$.

PDF p.4

Use the half-angle substitution $u = \tan(\theta/2)$, so that:

$$\sin\theta = \frac{2u}{1+u^2}, \quad \cos\theta = \frac{1-u^2}{1+u^2}$$

Substituting into the equations for $\xi$ and $\phi$, and using the constraint $\psi = \xi + \phi$, after algebraic manipulation (substituting and collecting terms), you arrive at a quartic polynomial in $u$ (degree 4 in $u = \tan(\theta/2)$).

A quartic equation has, in general, four roots, hence four solutions for $\theta$.

Special Condition for Two Solutions

When the coefficients of $u^3$ and $u$ in the quartic become zero, the polynomial reduces to a quadratic in $u^2$, yielding only two solutions for $\theta$. This occurs under a specific relationship among $a$, $b$, $c$, $d$, and $\psi$.

Answer: In the general case, the half-angle substitution produces a quartic in $u = \tan(\theta/2)$, giving four solutions. When the coefficients of $u^3$ and $u$ vanish, it reduces to a quadratic in $u^2$, giving just two solutions.

Problem 6

PDF p.4 20 marks
Problem Statement

A 4R manipulator is shown schematically. The nonzero link parameters are $\alpha_1 = -90°$, $d_2 = 1$, $\alpha_2 = 45°$, $d_3 = 1$, and $a_3 = 1$, and the mechanism is pictured in the configuration corresponding to $\Theta = [0, 0, 90°, 0]^\top$. Each joint has $\pm 180°$ as limits. Find all values of $\theta_3$ such that

$${}^{0}P_{4_{ORG}} = [1.29, 1.99, 0.0947]^\top$$
Figure: A 4R robot manipulator with DH frames shown. Frame $\{0,1\}$ is at the base with $\hat{Z}_{0,1}$ vertical and $\hat{X}_{0,1}$ horizontal. Frame $\{2\}$ is offset by $d_2$ along $\hat{Z}_2$. Frame $\{3\}$ has axes $\hat{X}_3$ and $\hat{Z}_3$ shown. Frame $\{4\}$ is at the end with $\hat{X}_4$ and $\hat{Z}_4$ shown. Cylindrical joints are depicted at each revolute axis.
PDF p.5

DH Parameters

ParameterValue
$\alpha_1 = -90°$$a_1 = 0$
$d_2 = 1$$d_4 = 0$
$\alpha_2 = 45°$$a_2 = 0$
$d_3 = 1$$a_3 = 1$

Configuration: $\Theta = [\theta_1, \theta_2, \theta_3, \theta_4]^\top = [0°, 0°, 90°, 0°]^\top$.

Step 1: Compute $r$ using Pieper's method

Using Slide 22 from Chapter 4, Item (1) (Pieper's method):

$$r = k_3$$

From the forward kinematics (Slide #21):

$${}^{0}P_{4_{ORG}} = \begin{bmatrix} C_1 g_1 - S_1 g_2 \\ S_1 g_1 + C_1 g_2 \\ g_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1.29 \\ 1.99 \\ 0.0947 \end{bmatrix}$$

Step 2: Compute $r$

$$r = x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = (1.29)^2 + (1.99)^2 + (0.0947)^2$$ $$\Rightarrow \boxed{r = 5.639}$$

Step 3: Compute $f_1$, $f_2$, $f_3$

$$f_1 = a_3 C_3 + d_4 S\alpha_3 S_3 + a_2 = C_3$$ $$f_2 = a_3 C\alpha_2 S_3 - d_4 S\alpha_3 C\alpha_2 C_3 - d_4 S\alpha_2 C\alpha_3 - d_3 S\alpha_2 = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}(S_3 - 1)$$ $$f_3 = a_3 S\alpha_2 S_3 - d_4 S\alpha_3 S\alpha_2 C_3 + d_4 C\alpha_2 C\alpha_3 + d_3 C\alpha_2 = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}(S_3 + 1)$$

Step 4: Compute $k_3$

$$f_1^2 + f_2^2 + f_3^2 = C_3^2 + \frac{1}{2}(S_3 - 1)^2 + \frac{1}{2}(S_3 + 1)^2$$ $$= C_3^2 + \frac{1}{2}S_3^2 - S_3 + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2}S_3^2 + S_3 + \frac{1}{2} = C_3^2 + S_3^2 + 1 = 2$$ $$k_3 = f_1^2 + f_2^2 + f_3^2 + a_2^2 + d_3^2 + 2f_2 d_3 + 2f_3 a_2$$

Substituting (noting from DH parameters, with the specific values plugged in):

$$k_3 = 3 + \sqrt{2}\,S_3 + \sqrt{2}$$ $$k_3 = 4.412 + 1.414\,S_3$$

Step 5: Equate and solve

Equating $r = k_3$:

$$5.639 = 4.412 + 1.414\,S_3$$ $$\Rightarrow S_3 = 0.866$$

Since $-180° < \theta_3 < 180°$:

$$\boxed{\theta_3 = 60° \quad \text{or} \quad \theta_3 = 120°}$$

Answer: The two values of $\theta_3$ that place the origin of frame $\{4\}$ at the given position are $\theta_3 = 60°$ and $\theta_3 = 120°$.
