         Definition of Inverse Kinematics

The inverse kinematics computes the values of the joint variables that would result to a
desired position or orientation of the end-effector.

Generally, it is desired for the manipulator to follow a particular path. For example, pick
up an object and place it elsewhere. Therefore, a numerical desired position and
orientation of the end-effector must be established.

Remarks:

• The desired position/orientation to be calculated for inverse kinematics should be
  with the workspace of the manipulator – which is based on the length of the links,
  joint kinematic layout, and joint limits.

• Redundant manipulators (more than 6-DoF) have an infinite number of solutions
  (configurations).

• Depending on the number of joints and the structure of the manipulator, the inverse
  kinematics may have no solutions, one solution, or multiple solutions.

• In the problem of inverse kinematics, normally, as set of nonlinear equations should
  be solved, for which, special attention should be given to the existence of the
  solution, number of solutions, and the method of solutions.

        Manipulator Workspace

The workspace of a robot manipulator is defined as the set of points that can be
reached by its end‐effector.

• The reachable workspace is a region that the manipulator can reach in at least one
  orientation.

• The dextrous workspace is a region that the manipulator can reach with all the
  orientations.

        4.11 Computational Considerations

In many path-controlled schemes (Chapter 7), it is necessary to calculate the inverse
kinematics of a manipulator at 30 Hz or faster. For computational efficiency, one may
consider the followings:

• Using a lookup-table for atan2 routine.

• Structure of the computation of multiple solutions in parallel (rather than pursuing
  them one after another serially).

• Using fixed- over floating-point representation of the quantities involved in
  calculation (it is possible since the variables do not have large dynamic range, and
  the ranges are fairly well-known).

• Factorization of equations to reduce the number of multiplications and additions –
  this comes at the cost of creating local variables (good trade-off)

• Avoid the computation of redundant kinematics, e.g., calculate only two columns of
  rotation, then compute the third column using cross product.

• For transcendental functions, they can be computed from a series expansion.
